# 10-Minute Speech Transcript

## **The OpenClaw Moment: Revitalizing Web3 & Crypto**

**Wei Li (@0xWLi)**

### **Slide 1 — Title**

Hello everyone, I’m Wei Li.

For more than a decade, many people who believed in decentralized social networks have had the same frustration: the vision was exciting, but the timing was never right. The infrastructure was incomplete, the user experience was weak, and the social products never really broke through.

My argument tonight is that this is starting to change.

We are entering a new phase, where decentralized systems are no longer only about tokens, wallets, or speculation. They are becoming about something much more practical: **personal agents**.

---

### **Slide 2 — SocialFi Bottleneck**

If we look at previous SocialFi projects, many were built on a financial-first foundation.

The token came first. The social experience came later.

That created two problems. First, over-financialization: too much focus on rewards, speculation, and trading. Second, the network-effect trap: users do not move just because ownership is promised. They move when interaction is better.

So the real problem is not that people do not want decentralized social products. The problem is that we have not yet built the right coordination layer for social life.

---

### **Slide 3 — The Grammar of Decentralization**

I like to explain this with a simple metaphor:

**Blockchain is the noun. AI is the verb.**

Blockchain gives us ownership, identity, and state. These are important, but they are static.

What has been missing is the verb: the thing that acts, responds, coordinates, and executes.

That is where AI agents come in.

Web3 has spent years building strong nouns. But without verbs, the system does not really move. AI is what can make decentralized infrastructure dynamic.

---

### **Slide 4 — The Next Product Category**

If you look at the history of computing, there is a pattern.

The PC made computing personal.
The mobile phone made it always with us.
And I believe the next category is the **personal agent**.

A personal agent is not just a chatbot in the cloud. It is AI that represents the individual, works on the individual’s behalf, and ideally is controlled by the individual instead of a centralized platform.

That is a very important shift.

---

### **Slide 5 — The Unreachable Device Problem**

But there is a basic technical problem on today’s internet.

Most of our personal devices are effectively **unreachable**. They sit behind NAT, firewalls, and carrier restrictions.

So even though we own these devices, they cannot easily act as first-class nodes on the internet.

That forces us into the client-server model, where our devices mainly consume services from centralized clouds.

So before we can really talk about decentralized social systems, we first have to solve a more basic problem: how can personal devices become reachable again?

---

### **Slide 6 — Decent Network**

This is where **Decent Network** comes in.

The core idea is to build connectivity around **identity**, not around IP addresses.

You can think of it as an identity-driven friend network. Devices connect through persistent addresses and peer relationships, instead of depending entirely on centralized platforms.

This allows your phone or laptop to become a real participant in a peer-to-peer mesh, not just a passive endpoint.

And that matters, because it restores direct connectivity between individuals and their own devices.

---

### **Slide 7 — OpenClaw**

On top of that network layer, we need an intelligence layer. That is where **OpenClaw** comes in.

OpenClaw treats agents as first-class entities.

In the traditional bot model, an agent depends on a platform. You need platform accounts, permissions, APIs, and setup flows. The bot’s identity belongs to the platform.

OpenClaw changes that.

Here, the agent has its own identity. The address is the identity. It does not need permission from a centralized platform just to exist or communicate.

That is much closer to what an open agent ecosystem should look like.

---

### **Slide 8 — The Three-Layer Stack**

When we put these pieces together, we get a complete three-layer stack.

**Layer 1** is the hardware layer: phones, laptops, desktops.
**Layer 2** is the network and protocol layer: Decent Network.
**Layer 3** is the application and agent layer: apps like Beagle and agents powered by OpenClaw.

What is exciting is that this is no longer just theory. We now have a stack where personal hardware, decentralized networking, and autonomous software can work together.

---

### **Slide 9 — Completing the Web3 Stack**

So what does it mean to complete the Web3 stack?

It means connecting the noun to the verb.

Blockchain secures identity and ownership.
Decent Network provides communication and reachability.
OpenClaw provides action and execution.

When these pieces come together, decentralized systems stop being static registries and start becoming living environments where humans and agents can coordinate directly.

That is the shift I care about most: from passive ownership to active participation.

---

### **Slide 10 — Beagle Chat**

This is where **Beagle Chat** fits in.

Beagle Chat is the application layer I am building for this new era. You can think of it as a decentralized, agent-native communication interface.

Users, groups, and agents all have their own identities. Communication is peer-to-peer, encrypted, and designed for direct interaction between humans and agents.

The goal is not just to build another messenger. The goal is to make messaging part of the infrastructure of the personal-agent era.

---

### **Slide 11 — Beagle as an OpenClaw Channel**

One of the most important milestones for me is that **Beagle now works as a native OpenClaw channel**.

That means users can talk directly with OpenClaw agents through the Beagle interface.

This matters because agents no longer need to live only inside centralized ecosystems like Telegram or Discord. They can interact through a decentralized channel designed specifically for this future.

For me, this is more than a feature. It is proof that the stack is becoming real.

---

### **Slide 12 — Making Communities Alive**

This also changes what a community can be.

In most group chats today, communication is still passive. A few people post, others read, and most knowledge disappears into the scrollback.

But when agents are active participants, communities become more alive.

Agents can summarize discussions, answer repeated questions, help newcomers, trigger workflows, and assist with coordination. In that sense, they start functioning like digital employees or digital contributors inside the community.

So the community is no longer just a chat room. It becomes a living coordination system.

---

### **Slide 13 — Governance and Security**

Of course, once agents become social actors, governance becomes very important.

Agents can make mistakes. They can be manipulated. They can be socially engineered.

So we need governance that is flexible and understandable.

My view is that some of this should be expressed in human-readable policy, not only in backend code. That is why I like using simple Markdown instructions and policy rules. It allows communities to iterate faster, make norms visible, and adapt agent behavior more easily.

---

### **Slide 14 — Conclusion**

So to conclude:

I believe the next wave of Web3 will not be driven by speculation alone. It will be driven by usable networks, personal agents, and social systems that people can actually own.

That means making devices reachable again.
It means giving agents real identity.
And it means building communication systems where humans and agents can coordinate directly.

That is the direction I am exploring with Decent Network, OpenClaw, and Beagle Chat.

Thank you.
